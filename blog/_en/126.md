# I Spent $880 Letting AI Build a Game From Scratch. It Made a Dud.

A while back I ran an experiment: without writing a single line of code, I'd let AI build me a shippable WeChat Mini Game, from zero.

The pitch was deliberately mundane — the kind of thing your mom forwards into the family group chat, where you tap to plant crops, watch ads to speed up the harvest, and rack up points you can trade for real eggs. A **farm game**. I know the genre cold, I know exactly how it makes money, and it's a perfect stress test for AI: clear requirements, clean boundaries, and a whole "plant → grow → watch ad → harvest → redeem" loop baked in.

A month later, it was done. It actually ran. It actually let you plant. It actually let you harvest.

Then I played it for five minutes, and the bill landed at **$880**...

I went quiet.

![The farm home screen it produced — at first glance, genuinely legit-looking](/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1783670742712-farm.png)

## 01 First, the glamour shot — and it really does fool you

Honestly, my first reaction was a little bit of awe.

Look at the image above — the wood-grain top bar, the little plaques for coins and harvest points, that level-shield badge in the top right, the nine freshly tilled plots below, a red barn off in the distance, tiny daisies at your feet... **everything you'd expect is there.** At a glance it reads as the real deal, tidier even than some outsourced work I've seen.

Tap an empty plot to sow. Tap a sprout to water. Crops grow in three stages (mound → sprout → ripe), harvesting has a sound effect, and a looping BGM plays the whole time. The UI transitions are genuinely **snappy**, a rock-solid 60fps.

The tech stack is no toy either: Cocos Creator 3.8.8, exporting straight to a WeChat Mini Game package. All the art is AI-generated, the BGM is AI-composed, the sound effects are synthesized locally, and even that wooden HUD skin was chroma-keyed out one piece at a time.

So — for something where "I didn't write a line of code," is this glamour shot worth the $880?

**Hold that thought.**

## 02 Then I played for five minutes — here's where the dud is

Time for the reality shot.

I idly opened the "Quests" page, and then I laughed —

![Every single line: go watch an ad](/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1783670748005-task.png)

"Watch 2 ads, +40." "Watch an ad for fertilizer." "Watch an ad for a speed-up card." "Missed a check-in? **Watch an ad to make it up.**"...

Then I tapped "Wheel," and oh boy: right under "1 Free Spin" sits a big blue button — "**Watch an Ad to Spin (3 left).**"

Back on the farm, I tapped a seedling, a popup appears, two buttons: "Water +25% (Free)" and "**Watch an Ad to Ripen Instantly (5 left).**"

![Plant it, tap it, and here comes another face full of watch-an-ad](/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1783670753557-ad-modal.png)

You read that right — the core loop of this game is, at its heart, **an orbit around the three words "watch an ad."** The AI took the "ad-placement design" table from the PRD and translated it, word for word, into a screen full of watch-ad buttons.

The problem is... **there are no actual ads wired in.** These "watch an ad" buttons, when you tap them, pop up a fake countdown overlay, spin for a few seconds, and hand you a reward. Real integration means swapping in `wx.createRewardedVideoAd` — but that's a story for later. Right now it's just an **empty shell.** A shell with "monetization" carved into its DNA that hasn't yet grown the ability to actually monetize.

The kicker is that "Growing..." popup (the image above). I wanted to flip over to another page, so I tapped outside the popup — **won't close.** No close button, and it doesn't respond to tapping the overlay. I was genuinely stuck in that popup, couldn't switch tabs, forced to pick between "Water" and "Watch an Ad."

(Sit with that interaction design for a second — isn't it maybe a little *too* considerate toward the advertisers...)

## 03 «Real Farm Produce», locked away somewhere you'll never reach

What's the killer hook of a farm game? **You can trade for real stuff.** Watch ads, bank points, and eventually they mail you 6 farm-fresh eggs — that alone is enough to keep grandmas and grandpas farming for a year.

So I eagerly opened the "Market":

![Real farm produce: 6 eggs, a veggie bundle, a fruit box, a grain gift set... and every one of them greyed out](/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1783670765375-market.png)

¥5 produce voucher — "Not enough harvest points."
¥10 fruit voucher — "Unlocks at Level 3."
6 farm-fresh eggs — "**Unlocks at Level 10**," plus 1,500 harvest points + 20 egg shards.
Grain gift set — "Unlocks at Level 20," 5,000 harvest points + 60 shards.

And me? A level-1 account with 0 harvest points and a daily cap of 300. Do the math yourself on how many (not-yet-integrated) ads you'd have to watch and how many days you'd have to farm to hit Level 10 and bank 1,500 points and 20 shards.

These numbers weren't pulled out of thin air by the AI — they're the anti-abuse rules I wrote down in black and white in my PRD: "New accounts can only redeem physical goods after 7 days," "one redemption per month max." The AI implemented them faithfully. **So faithfully that it locked a game's single most tempting feature firmly away in a future no player will ever reach.**

It executed flawlessly. It just never realized that, locked down like this, the game doesn't have one single minute of *fun* in it.

## 04 Even the friends are fake

Next I opened "Me."

![Xiaoming, Ah-hua, Old Wang — your three plastic friends](/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1783670770606-me.png)

"Friend Assist (water for harvest points)" lists three people: Xiaoming, Ah-hua, and Old Wang.

I stared at "Old Wang" for a solid three seconds.

These three are **fake people hardcoded into the source.** No social system, no WeChat friend graph, not a single living human. Just three hardcoded names with a default avatar, standing there acting out a little play called "You Have Friends."

The unlock-progression column is the same: Lv3 tomatoes, Lv5 daily orders, Lv8 the wheel, Lv10 real redemptions, Lv20 co-ops... all laid out crisply, planned to look the part. But behind it there isn't a single reason that makes you *want* to grind to Lv10. **It built the game's skeleton with meticulous care, and only forgot to tuck a soul inside.**

This is what I mean by dud — not that it won't run, but that it's **hollow.** Like a fully furnished model apartment: all the furniture's there, the lighting's cozy, but the second you push the door open you just know — nobody lives here, and nobody could.

## 05 The war stories: half of that $880 burned on "chroma-keying"

Let's get technical, to prove I actually got my hands dirty (and to explain where the money actually went).

The art came from Zhipu GLM-Image, and it was potholes the whole way:

- **The `--ref` parameter is flat-out ignored.** No way to feed it a reference image, so to keep dozens of assets stylistically consistent, I had to **repeat** the same style description (palette, outlines, lighting...) in every single prompt, holding it together like a broken record.
- **Every image has an "AI-generated" watermark welded onto the bottom-right corner**, and the CLI can't strip it. Zoom in on the "Coins x120" tile on the wheel, or those vouchers in the market — **the noise and watermark leftovers are plain as day.** The only way to get clean assets is to crop them afterward, or switch providers.
- **Chroma-keying was a disaster.** It promised a green screen, but the model stubbornly refused to give you a pure green background, so you had to sample the real corner color first and then manually pass the key. Green vegetables would get keyed right out along with the green screen, so I switched the backdrop to magenta `#FF00FF`. Brown soil? Push despill too hard and it fades to a greyish green, so you have to clamp it down hard to 0.1...

![Chroma-key any one of these and that "AI-generated" watermark in the bottom-right just won't shake off](/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1783670774625-ai-art.png)

The engine side took its share of punishment too:

- Cocos's nine-slice (SLICED) squishes narrow buttons into pointy **"almond shapes,"** so you're forced back to plain stretched sprites, which means the button art *has* to be drawn as a flat-edged rounded rectangle — **stretch a capsule shape and it deforms.**
- The headless build **only registers changes to PNG content**; edit the meta and it plays dumb, so you have to re-save the PNG to "trick" it into re-importing.
- That compressed uuid (cid) in the scene JSON is brutally fragile to hand-edit — nudge it and the scene unbinds from the script, so it has to be script-generated.
- Screenshotting with a headless browser to verify: without `--enable-unsafe-swiftshader` you get a straight-up **white screen** — shader compilation just died.

The AI hit every one of these potholes, and filled every one of them in, one by one. It genuinely can do the work. **It's just that every pothole it filled was burning my tokens.** Regenerate art, tweak the UI, re-key and re-align — over and over. That $880 mostly went up in smoke in exactly this kind of loop that "looks like progress but is really just polishing the same screw in place."

## 06 So — it hasn't hit its iPhone moment yet

I have to be fair here: **this task was unthinkable three years ago.**

One person, one sentence, one month, zero code, out comes a mini-game skeleton with a complete tech stack, self-consistent art, a real WeChat package, and a fully closed gameplay loop. That's already magic. The AI took the task of "translate a PRD into a running app" and nailed it to a 95.

But a game isn't a PRD translation exercise.

A game is about that last 5%: is the core loop fun, do the numbers feel good, is there an addictive tactile hook, when does it hand you an unexpected surprise — **the stuff that makes people unable to put the phone down.** And that 5% is precisely the part the AI right now has **absolutely no concept of.** It can make the "Watch an Ad to Ripen Instantly" button pixel-perfect, and yet have no idea that a good game shouldn't make you watch an ad at every single step.

It reminds me of smartphones before the iPhone.

The devices back then had every spec box ticked: make calls, browse the web, install apps, get email — pull up the feature list and it was longer than the iPhone's. And yet you just didn't want to use them, because they "had everything, they just weren't good to use." Then the iPhone showed up — not by adding a pile of features, but by being **suddenly so good you couldn't leave it.**

AI making games is stuck right there, in the "pre-iPhone" slot: **it has everything, it's just not fun.**

Its iPhone moment is the day you ask it to make a game and what it hands back isn't a feature-complete skeleton, but something you **can't help wanting to play just one more round of.** That day is coming. But at least this time, what my $880 bought was a beautifully crafted dud —

along with a rock-solid conclusion: **AI can already stand a game up for you, but the word "fun" — that, for now, you still have to teach it yourself.**

(Old Wang, next time I promise I'll get you a real body.)
