# Tear Down an AC, a TV, and a Console, Then Topple 1095 Dominoes: 4 three.js Sites in One Day

Scrolling through GitHub yesterday, I did a double take at my own account.

On July 17, four new repos showed up: 15:06, 15:07, 15:07, 19:03.

The first three were pushed **within the same minute**…

They are: an air conditioner that breaks down into 33 parts, a 21-layer OLED TV, a 20-piece white gaming console, and a soccer pitch stacked with **1095 giant dominoes**.

All four are interactive websites you can play with right now — every one of them is live.

Crazier still, here's what they have in common: **not a single 3D model file, not a single texture image loaded.**

Every part is pieced together from three.js primitive geometry, every texture is painted live on Canvas, and even three.js itself is vendored into a local directory — the whole site works with the network unplugged.

(3D artists reading this can relax — your jobs are safe for now… probably.)

Let me tear them down for you, one by one.

## 01 ARCTIC BREEZE, or: Taking an Appliance Apart, Properly

The first site is called "ARCTIC BREEZE" — a fictional 1.5-HP inverter split air conditioner, heating and cooling.

<video src="/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545700947-air-assembly.mp4" controls playsinline preload="metadata" style="width:100%;border:1px solid #e5e5e5;border-radius:8px"></video>

Open the page and it starts assembling itself: outdoor unit → through the wall → indoor unit → piping → remote control, 33 parts flying into place one after another, with the camera tracking the assembly as it goes.

Once it's built, one click blows it apart — every part scatters along its own vector, the wall automatically turns translucent, and bilingual labels float next to the parts on little leader lines.

![Exploded view of all 33 parts, the wall gone translucent](/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545677146-air-explode-still.png)

But my favorite part is **powering it on for a test run**:

<video src="/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545704972-air-run.mp4" controls playsinline preload="metadata" style="width:100%;border:1px solid #e5e5e5;border-radius:8px" poster="/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545693317-air-cover.png"></video>

The cross-flow fan spins up, the louver sweeps back and forth, and blue cold air sinks to the floor and spreads out — yes, cold air sinks, and it modeled even that.

Tap heating mode and everything reverses: warm air indoors, and the outdoor unit switches to blowing orange hot exhaust.

That's not two separate animations — that's **the four-way valve earning its keep**. This fake AC's refrigeration cycle is, logically speaking, real.

(Also, there are night and rain toggles. An outdoor unit quietly grinding away in a midnight downpour looks a little pitiful, and a little romantic…)

## 02 POLAR NIGHT, or: An OLED Is a Mille-Feuille

The second site, "POLAR NIGHT", is a 65-inch 4K OLED sitting in a complete living room: TV stand, sofa, coffee table, rug, floor lamp, and a city with rain outside the window.

21 parts, built up layer by layer starting from the OLED panel: panel → ribbon cables → T-CON board → graphite heat-spreader film → mid-frame → backplate → circuit boards → speakers → rear cover → stand.

<video src="/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545708199-oled-explode.mp4" controls playsinline preload="metadata" style="width:100%;border:1px solid #e5e5e5;border-radius:8px"></video>

The exploded view fans out toward the viewer — and there's something genuinely striking about the moment that mille-feuille of an OLED stack spreads itself flat.

What really earns the ticket price is **movie mode**:

<video src="/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545761874-oled-watch.mp4" controls playsinline preload="metadata" style="width:100%;border:1px solid #e5e5e5;border-radius:8px" poster="/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545680015-oled-cover.png"></video>

Power it on and the screen starts playing moving footage (an aurora demo reel, test color bars, or a synthwave-style racing game), and the whole room lights up from the screen.

That light is not baked into a texture — it's a three.js RectAreaLight. The screen is actually emitting light, and the light actually spills onto the sofa and the rug.

Pair it with the night toggle and you've got an OLED commercial, shot live on set.

In game mode, little pulses of signal even run down the HDMI cable… (Whoever came up with that detail, step forward and accept your compliments.)

## 04 The Domino Pitch, or: All 1095 Dominoes Fall for Real

The first three sites are, admittedly, three runs of the same playbook: assemble, explode, power on.

The fourth one takes a hard turn.

A regulation soccer pitch (105 × 68 meters), stacked with **1095 giant dominoes, each 2.2 meters tall**, along a route roughly 1.1 kilometers long.

<video src="/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545811748-domino-full.mp4" controls playsinline preload="metadata" style="width:100%;border:1px solid #e5e5e5;border-radius:8px" poster="/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545696856-domino-cover.png"></video>

Here's the headline: **not one frame of it is scripted animation.**

Every domino is a real rigid body in the Rapier physics engine (WASM). You push the first one, and the other 1094 get knocked over one by one by actual physics — the wavefront travels at about 4.6 m/s, matching the theoretical domino speed √gh.

And the route runs in three dimensions:

- The bottom half of the pitch snakes back and forth, a fork branches off into a **spiral** in the center circle, and the spiral's tail dives in to smash a crosshatch-stacked tower;
- The dominoes climb **11 steps of stairs** and cross a **4.6-meter-high skybridge**;
- The last domino on the bridge **dives off**, hits a catch bed on the ground, and the relay carries on;
- Then comes a 15×11 domino field, where a V-shaped wave whooshes outward;
- And the final domino knocks loose a giant soccer ball that rolls into the goal — confetti, and the scoreboard flashes GOAL.

![The skybridge section: dominoes relaying across a deck 4.6 meters up](/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545686997-domino-stairs-still.png)

<video src="/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545817540-domino-goal.mp4" controls playsinline preload="metadata" style="width:100%;border:1px solid #e5e5e5;border-radius:8px"></video>

You don't have to follow the script, either: click any domino to knock it over in the direction you're looking and start your own fork; or hit "multi-push" and six segments start their waves at once — the whole pitch goes down in 30 seconds.

<video src="/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545821928-domino-multi.mp4" controls playsinline preload="metadata" style="width:100%;border:1px solid #e5e5e5;border-radius:8px"></video>

Engineering-wise, this site has three details I really like:

**First, 1095 dominoes cost exactly 1 draw call.** All of them live inside a single InstancedMesh, and every frame the rigid-body poses get written back into the instance matrices.

**Second, the physics knows how to sleep.** All 1095 rigid bodies are asleep by default; only the dominoes near the wavefront are awake, so a physics step takes about 1ms — which is why 8x speed doesn't stutter.

**Third, before launch it ran the entire simulation headless in node.** No browser — pure physics stepping to verify everything: the route connects end to end, the big field falls completely, the tower gets smashed apart, goal at 170.7 seconds. With a domino run, one dead tile anywhere and the whole show is over — you can't gamble that on eyeballing it.

(Route generation is fun too: turtle-graphics straight lines and arcs, plus Bézier fork ramps and an Archimedean spiral, with a self-check at generation time on the minimum spacing between non-adjacent dominoes, to prevent "toppling across the aisle"…)

![The finish: the last domino launches a soccer ball into the goal, scoreboard reads GOAL](/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545690093-domino-goal-still.png)

## 05 Zero Assets, or: Why Not a Single Texture File

The four sites add up to 8298 lines of core code: AC 2545, TV 2283, console 2149, dominoes 1321 (including HTML/CSS, excluding vendor).

No .glb, no .png assets, no CDN.

The AC's energy-efficiency label, the aurora playing on the TV, the pitch's turf and line markings, the crowd in the stands, the ad boards, the scoreboard — all of it is procedurally painted on Canvas, live.

The constraint felt like self-flagellation at first. The actual payoff was huge:

- **Each site is just a handful of JS files** — throw it on any static host and it runs, offline included;
- Changing a part doesn't mean a trip back to an artist's desk — tweak two parameters and refresh;
- Each site even ships a URL-parameter screenshot interface — `?p=1&x=0.6&view=explode` drops you straight into a 60% exploded view. Every illustration in this post was batch-generated by script.

Oh, and deployment had one gotcha: on this machine, wrangler gets wedged by the proxy's TUN mode, so in the end we bypassed wrangler entirely and uploaded straight through the Cloudflare Pages REST API. (Even the deploy script grew on its own — deploy-cf.cjs, one copy in each of the four repos.)

## 06 So

One day, 4 sites, from "take an air conditioner apart" to "topple 1095 dominoes".

Looking back, all four sites are answering the same question: **how low can the floor for an interactive website go?**

No art assets, no modeling software, no game-engine editor — armed with nothing but geometry primitives, Canvas, and a physics engine, how far can "a thing you can actually play with" really get?

The answer: the AC powers on and cools, the TV lights up a living room, the console's fans track its temperature, and 1095 dominoes genuinely carry the wave from the very first tile all the way into the goal.

That used to be called a team's sprint plan.

Now it's called yesterday.
