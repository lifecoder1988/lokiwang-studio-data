Tonight at 18:04, I said my first sentence to Claude Code.

At 20:01, a complete game — 246 files — landed in git.

## 01 The One-Sentence Kickoff, or: 54 Minutes Later There Was an Actual Game

18:04, sentence one:

> Manage this project with wing cli. Implement the game Slay the Spire. Use the 2d game skill in my-skills (codex image generation), use the local godot as the engine.

That's it. No card count, no art direction, not even a word about "how far to take it."

At 18:58, it replied: **"Done. The Slay the Spire Godot remake is fully playable."**

Fifty-four minutes. I had been planning to scroll my phone for a bit.

And it didn't slap together a demo to call it a day — all the engineering hygiene was there: wing-managed domain docs (the combat math formulas written into rules.md, labeled "the single source of truth for the code"), an architecture decision record for the engine choice, an OpenSpec change proposal, a test suite with ~3,800 assertions, and `wing check --strict` passing with 0 errors, 0 warnings.

(Honestly, more disciplined than I am when I start a new project…)

## 03 "Play It Yourself," or: It Wrote Itself a Stand-In

Sentence three, from me: "Play it yourself and see."

What I expected: it launches the game, takes two screenshots, says everything's fine.

What it understood: **write a spire-climbing bot that drives the real UI** — simulating card clicks, target selection, reading enemy intents to decide between blocking and attacking, resting when low, fighting elites when healthy, opening chests, shopping — then play 6 full runs, plus 100 engine-level boss simulations.

Sixteen minutes later it dropped a Playtest Report on me. The battle log:

| Run | Result | Cause of death / highlight |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | 💀 Floor 3 | Ignored the cultist, who stacked Ritual to 21 Strength and hit for 27 — snowballed to death (mechanics correct; the bot just can't play) |
| 2 | 💀 Floor 4 | Limped out of the last fight with 8 HP, every branch was a monster room, died in a forced fight |
| 3 | 💀 Boss | Full 7-floor run, 6 straight wins, challenged the Guardian at 65 HP and fell short |
| 4 | 💀 Boss | Walked into the boss room at 48 HP, lost |
| 5 | 💀 Floor 6 | Got greedy: upgraded twice at a campfire at 52 HP instead of healing |
| 6 | 🏆 Victory | Picked up Inflame + Demon Form, **killed the boss with 2 HP left** |

The playtest also caught and fixed 3 real bugs along the way. For that run-1 death it even added a note: "the mechanics are completely correct; the bot just doesn't know how to play" — **losing and blaming your teammate. Very esports.**

That 62-second video at the top of this post? Same bot. It died again that run: barely survived an elite fight on floor 5 with 6 HP, then fell on floor 6. One win in six runs — the tuning apparently inherited some of Slay the Spire's actual temperament…

## 04 Four Pointed Questions, or: It Had Been Making a Silent Film All Along

The first version looked impressive, but after two hands of cards something felt off. So sentence four was four questions:

> 1. Does the game have animations right now 2. Sound effects 3. Background music 4. Is the HUD AI-generated

Its answers were surprisingly honest: animations exist but are basic; **sound effects — none at all, there isn't a single AudioStreamPlayer in the project**; BGM — also none, on any screen; HUD — the icons are generated art, the health bars and buttons are drawn in code.

In other words, those two hands I just played? A **silent film**. Playing a card: silent. Getting hit: silent. The boss winding up a hammer to flatten me: also silent.

(To its credit, it hasn't learned the "this is minimalist sound design" line. Ask, and it tells you what's missing.)

Sentence five, three words: "Do all of it."

## 05 "Do All of It," or: numpy Composed 4 Background Tracks by Hand

The next 20 minutes were my favorite part of the whole evening.

For sound effects, it didn't hunt for an asset pack or call a generation API — **it procedurally synthesized 16 sound effects with numpy**: two tiers of hit impacts, a metallic block clang, card-play and card-draw swooshes, coin jingles, a victory arpeggio, a descending defeat triad… deterministic generation, zero API keys.

The BGM went further: **the same script straight-up composed music** — 4 seamless loops, compressed to OGG with ffmpeg. The main menu is a 70 bpm dark chord pad with sparse bells, the map is wind ambience, combat is a 100 bpm bassline with a drum kit, and the boss fight gets its own track.

(A programmer, press-ganged into being the in-house composer. Asset budget: $0.)

The animations got filled in too: sword-slash arcs (procedural textures, additive blending), cards flying on draw, black-fade screen transitions, idle breathing on the portraits. Damage even got tiered audio — anything over 15 automatically switches to the heavy-hit sound.

At 19:53 it announced: "All four items complete. `godot --path .` for the version with sight and sound."

## 07 The Debrief, or: Numbers and a Thought

The ledger for these two hours:

- **What I said**: 7 sentences, under 150 characters
- **What it did**: 281 tool calls, roughly 1.2 million output tokens
- **What came out**: 246 files — the GDScript game itself, 60 Codex-generated images, 16 SFX + 4 BGM tracks (numpy-synthesized), ~3,800 test assertions, plus the full domain-docs + ADR + OpenSpec set
- **Failures**: 1 (image-generation rate limit), self-resolved by retrying
- **Rework**: 0 — not one of my sentences was spent fixing something it got wrong

Looking back at the 7 sentences, only two actually earned their keep: "play it yourself," and the four-question audit. The first forced it to write a bot, play its own game 6 times, and fix 3 bugs. The second forced it to admit it had shipped a silent film.

The other five were, functionally, "hm, keep going."

We used to say the bottleneck in programming was writing the code. Then we said it was the requirements. Now, apparently, **the bottleneck is remembering to ask: what about the sound?**
