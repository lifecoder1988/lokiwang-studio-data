# Why Does ChatGPT Type One Word at a Time? I Built a 3D "Token Foundry" to Show You

You've seen how ChatGPT types.

One word. Then another. Popping out.

A lot of people assume it's a "typewriter effect" — a bit of theater to look profound…

It isn't. It really is **computing one word at a time**. Every word that pops out means the model has run one complete pass through its entire pipeline — and before the next word can start, it first has to swallow the one it just produced.

I've been wanting to explain this to my normal, non-tech friends forever, but every time I get within three terms of "autoregressive" or "KV cache," their eyes start to glaze over…

So tonight I tried a different approach: if I can't explain it, I'll **build it**.

I had Claude use three.js to turn LLM inference and training into a 3D factory. Words are the raw material, the building is the model, glowing orbs are the data flow — treat it like touring a real-estate scale model. Finish this post, and your understanding of LLMs will probably beat 90% of people.

It's called **TOKEN FOUNDRY** — 炼词厂, the "word foundry." Claude named it itself, and I refuse to change a single character.

## 02 Inference: One Word Runs the Entire Factory

Inference first — this is what it's doing every day when you use ChatGPT:

<video src="/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545930542-inference.mp4" controls playsinline preload="metadata" style="width:100%;border:1px solid #e5e5e5;border-radius:8px" poster="/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545917755-inf-done.png"></video>

The scene is simple: a patch of factory ground, a row of colored **word tiles** up front, a translucent 6-story building behind them.

The tiles are tokens — the model doesn't see "sentences," only tiles standing in line. The opening prompt is four tiles: 人工 / 智能 / 正在 / 改变 — "artificial / intelligence / is / changing."

The building is the model itself. Each floor runs two work stations:

- **Self-attention**: the word looks up and back at every word before it, deciding "whose work should I weight the most" — think of a new hire walking into the shop and first flipping through every senior worker's logbook;
- **Feed-forward network (MLP)**: back at its own bench, head down, kneading everything it just gathered into its own representation.

Then comes my favorite part of the whole video: the complete process of generating one new word.

A glowing orb rises above the last tile in the queue, dives into the ground floor, and climbs floor by floor — at each level it pauses first at the attention station (in 3D you can see it fan a sheaf of arcs backward: that's it "looking back" at the history), then pauses at the feed-forward station, then heads upstairs.

![Orb mid-climb: the arcs are the current word's attention to past words](/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545897590-inf-attn.png)

At the very top sits the **output head (LM head)** — the scoring desk, which grades every single word in the vocabulary. Five probability bars slam upright on the roof:

![The output head raises the top-5 candidate probability bars for the next word](/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545900590-inf-bars.png)

Say the word at the end of the queue is "changing" — the candidates are: world 40%, future 22%, everything 16%, us 12%, life 10%.

Then — roll the dice. The winning word traces a parabola off the roof and **flies back to the end of the queue**, becoming the new last tile.

One word, out of the oven.

And then? The orb lifts off again and climbs from floor one all over again. The end of the queue has changed, so everything starts from scratch…

This is **autoregressive** generation: every word runs the entire factory, and when it's done, the product gets shoved back into the raw-material queue. When you watch ChatGPT ticking out word after word, that's not an effect — that's **the factory shipping one delivery at a time**.

(The scoreboard reads 0.3 tok/s — so you can see every station, this is probably the slowest LLM on Earth. Real models do tens or hundreds of tokens a second. Same exact pipeline.)

## 03 The Pause: That Half-Second You Wait, It's Taking Notes

Ever noticed that after ChatGPT receives your question, it always **pauses a beat** before it starts typing?

The longer the question, the longer the pause.

The demo acts out why. At the start of the video there's a phase called **prefill**: all four prompt tiles press into the building at once, and the six floors light up from bottom to top — every prompt word passes through the model **in parallel**.

![PREFILL complete: KV cache built, 4 positions × 6 layers](/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545903934-inf-prefill.png)

This pass produces no new words at all. So what is it doing?

**Taking notes.**

Each floor holds a 16×6 "memory lattice," and for every word processed, a copy of that word's notes at that layer gets filed into the matching cell — the term of art is the **KV cache**.

From then on, every time a new word is generated and looks back at history, it doesn't have to recompute the earlier text — it just flips through the notes. Which is why that sheaf of attention arcs actually connects to the memory lattice, not to the tiles themselves.

That half-second you wait is the model reading through your whole question, taking notes layer by layer. Only once the notes are done does it start writing the answer, one word at a time.

(Incidentally, this is also why long conversations get pricier and slower the longer they run — the notebook keeps thickening, and every new word has to flip through all of it…)

## 04 The Dice: Temperature Is Just Nerve

I said "roll the dice" — and the dice are tunable. That's the mysterious **temperature** parameter in every AI app.

The panel offers four settings: greedy, 0.7, 1.0, 1.3.

- **Greedy (T=0)**: no dice at all — always take the #1 probability. Steady, but liable to say "Artificial intelligence is changing the world." ten thousand times in a row;
- **Temperature up**: the probability distribution gets "flattened," and long-shot words get a chance at the throne. At 1.3, the model visibly starts to let loose…

The demo also hides a "hand of God": during the second-and-change that the probability bars stand upright, you can click any bar in the 3D scene and force the model to pick that word.

I recommend clicking the last-place candidate every single time and personally driving a model into incoherence — you'll feel in your bones how much sampling strategy matters (this is a legitimate pedagogical suggestion, really).

## 05 Training: The Factory Runs Night School

Inference done, one question remains: what gives that scoring desk on the roof the right to hand "world" 40 points and "life" 10?

The scores come from **training**. Switch to training mode, and the factory goes on night shift:

<video src="/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545942844-training.mp4" controls playsinline preload="metadata" style="width:100%;border:1px solid #e5e5e5;border-radius:8px" poster="/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545921422-trn-forward.png"></video>

The lot gets a fresh batch of raw material: 4 sentences of corpus, 8 tiles each. The corpus itself is very "textbook" — "large models learn patterns from massive corpora," "backpropagation carries the error back into every layer's weights"… The lessons used for training are about training itself. Slightly recursive.

Every training step is a four-beat combo:

**Beat one: sit the exam (forward pass).** Every word in the batch presses into the building **at once**, a green wave sweeping up through all six floors — note the difference from inference: inference is one word per trip, training is the whole class going through together. This is why training takes a room full of GPUs.

**Beat two: grade the answers (loss).** The exam question for a language model is blunt: cover up the next word of each sentence and make the model guess. ✓s and ✗s light up across the roof, and the scoreboard posts the **cross-entropy loss** — a single number for "how outrageously wrong was the class, on average." First score: 4.895. Brutal.

![STEP 002: loss 4.895, a roofline of ✗s](/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545907652-trn-loss.png)

**Beat three: assign blame (backpropagation).** The best shot in the entire video: an orange wave sweeps from the roof down to the ground floor — **backwards** — throwing sparks the whole way. The error travels down from the output layer, level by level, and every floor gets told "here's how much of this mistake is on you, and which direction to adjust."

![Backpropagation: the orange wave pours down from the top floor, gradient sparks flying](/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545911051-trn-backprop.png)

**Beat four: rewrite the error notebook (weight update).** The whole building flashes white: θ ← θ − η·∇θ. In human terms: every parameter takes one tiny step in the direction of "be slightly less wrong next time."

Then? Repeat. Sit the exam, grade the answers, assign blame, rewrite the notebook. One step at a time.

There's a small curve in the top-right of the scoreboard — watch it fall from a cliff into a gentle slope: after 60 steps, the loss drops from 5.10 to 0.97, the ✗s thin out, the ✓s line up in rows —

![Converged after 60 steps: loss 0.973, the model has "learned" the corpus patterns](/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784545914718-trn-done.png)

That is the entire daily routine of "training a large model." In the real world, this four-beat combo repeats a few million steps across tens of thousands of GPUs, burning enough electricity to power a small city for a while… But what each individual step does is exactly the four beats you just watched.

## 06 The Gibberish: Where "In a By-the-Way Manner" Came From

Confession time.

At the end of the inference video, this factory — with a perfectly straight face — generated a sentence:

> Artificial intelligence is changing our rules, in a by-the-way manner.

…What kind of manner is "a by-the-way manner"?

For the record: this is not a bug, this is **honesty**. There is not a single real neural network weight anywhere in the TOKEN FOUNDRY — it runs on a hand-written probability table, a "word chain" of 30-odd words: who can follow whom, at what probability, all hard-coded in an 87-line JS file. The loss curve and the hit rates are hand-rolled stage props too, modeled on what real training looks like.

That "32K vocab" bragged on the factory sign? Actual inventory: 30 words. It has the false-advertising act down cold as well…

But every beat of the pipeline — prefill, layer-by-layer attention, KV cache, probability distribution, sample-and-refill, forward, loss, backward, update — is drawn in the exact order the real thing runs.

And that bit of gibberish — "in a by-the-way manner" — happens to be the one thing this post most wants to say:

A 30-word probability table already generates sentences that are **sentence-shaped but not human**.

Now swap the 30-word vocabulary for 150,000, swap the hand-written probability table for hundreds of billions of trained parameters, swap 60 training steps for trillions of tokens — and the gibberish upgrades, step by step, into something that can write your year-end review.

**The principle never changed. Only the scale did.** That may be the single most important sentence for understanding LLMs.
