# Three Trips to the Divorce Registry in Ten Years. Zero Divorces.

Let's get one thing straight up front: **they did not get divorced.**

That sentence has to go first. Because the headlines you've been scrolling past these last few days mostly say the opposite.

I believed them too, at first. Until I went and checked.

## 01 He Beat the Whole World

August 24, 2008. Beijing Olympics, men's boxing, 48 kg final. Zou Shiming beat his Mongolian opponent and took the gold.

August 2012, London, 49 kg. He won again. **Back-to-back Olympic champion.**

November 6, 2016, Las Vegas. At 35, he beat the Thai boxer Prasitsak Phaprom and took the WBO flyweight world championship belt.

![Two Olympic golds and one world championship belt](/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784121430365-cover.jpg)

That was the peak of his career.

July 28, 2017, Shanghai Oriental Sports Center. Title defense, against Japan's Sho Kimura. At 2 minutes 28 seconds into round 11, Zou Shiming didn't make it to the final bell.

That was his last fight. He was 36.

## 02 He Lost to the Business World

After the ring came business. A high-end boxing gym, restaurants, livestream selling — the couple had over a dozen companies tied to their names.

Seven years.

According to Jimu News, and to Ran Yingying's own account on the variety show *Sisters in Charge 2*: **seven straight years of losses, nearly 200 million yuan in debt.**

![Seven years, nearly 200 million yuan in losses](/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784121436135-gym.jpg)

I went and verified that number myself. The internet is also circulating "lost 700 million," "abandoned her twenty-three times," "blind in the left eye" — **only content-farm accounts are spreading those, the versions contradict each other, and I'm not writing down a single one.**

What can actually be put on paper is just this: nearly 200 million, seven years, a dozen-plus companies.

A man who beat the entire world at 48 kilograms spent seven years getting beaten in a ring he never learned.

## 03 Ten Years, Three Trips

What actually pushed this onto the trending charts was Ran Yingying's own account on *Sisters in Charge 2*.

She said that over ten years, she walked into the civil affairs bureau three times.

**All three times, the divorce didn't happen.**

![She stands holding a red folder; he hangs back in the background, head down](/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784121441097-bureau.jpg)

Why not?

One reason is brutally practical: with over a dozen companies tied to both their names, the assets and debts had long since tangled into one knot — **there was simply no clean way to split them.** To divorce, they'd first have to pull 200 million apart. It wouldn't come apart.

The other reason she stated even more plainly herself: she grew up in a single-parent family, and she didn't want her three sons growing up in a broken one too.

On the show she scored the marriage a **five** (out of ten). A failing grade.

She said one more thing: **"More debt than love."**

## 04 She Carried It

These are details from public reports. I'm listing them as-is, no commentary:

- Separate bedrooms, three years.
- Day-to-day conversation reduced to almost nothing but the debt.
- Multiple properties across four cities, sold off one by one to repay it.
- She now rents, with the three kids.
- She livestreams to sell goods, often until the small hours.
- The three boys' education and all the housework — carried by her alone, for years.

On the show, her name for this was a "widow-style marriage."

Reading this far, you've probably already picked a side.

…But this is where the story takes a turn.

## 05 On Their Fifteenth Anniversary

February 8, 2026. Their fifteenth wedding anniversary.

Each of them posted something.

Zou Shiming wrote:

> **"15 years. Three meals, four seasons, one life together."**

Ran Yingying wrote:

> **"With this home and all of you, life is sweet. Defense is my punch."**

Defense is my punch.

A boxer's wife, stating her choice in boxing terms: **don't attack. Hold the line.**

![Properties in four cities sold off; two people decide to carry it to the end together](/api/media/uploads/2026/07/1784121446034-together.jpg)

Three walks to the registry's door, three turnarounds. In year fifteen, on the same day, both of them said it publicly: we're not divorcing.

Jimu News wrote one line of commentary on this, and I think it lands: **once the glory fades, shouldering the load is the last card a marriage holds.**

I'm not going to draw any conclusions about their marriage. Five out of ten is five out of ten; more debt than love is more debt than love. She said those things herself, and I have no right to prettify them for her.

I just think that a man who once fought a championship belt back from Las Vegas, and a woman who livestreams into the small hours, choosing to pay off 200 million together —

**that, in itself, is a far more interesting story than the word "divorce."**
